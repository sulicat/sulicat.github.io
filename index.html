The Resistance of the Working Class to the Industrial Revolution

 The industrial revolution is a time in history between 1760 and 1820 when a large section of the world (Europe and US) saw a massive rise in the use of machinery for manufacturing. The US saw a large increase in population during that time too. One of the characteristics of the Industrial Revolution is that lots of people worked in factories. It is known that a lot of the working conditions in factories were abhorrent, from low wages and discrimination to extremely unsafe conditions in the workplace. There was a lot of resistance demonstrated by the working class (In the form of protests). Was that resistance justifiable? Was life in fact better for the working class than we may believe? There are some aspects of life that did improve, notably the price of goods decreasing and the wider availability of goods due to mass production.

 The story "Sweat" describes the life of Delia Jones, written in the 1920s it is a fictional story where Delia is hard working woman living a little after the second industrial revolution.  We can also see that the working conditions for Delia are not as bad as the working conditions for people in large cities during the Industrial revolution, Delia is a washwoman, she does not have to work in a factory around dangerous equipment and unhygienic conditions. Research

 There are many people who talk about the pros and cons of the Industrial Revolution, John P. Rafferty, an Editor at Encyclopedia Britannica mentions many of these. He mentions the cost of goods decreasing "Factories and the machines that they housed began to produce items faster and cheaper than could be made by hand. As the supply of various items rose, their cost to the consumer declined" (Rafferty) and also talks about an enhanced quality of life for the average person, "Mass production lowered the costs of much-needed tools, clothes, and other household items for the common (that is, non aristocratic) people, which allowed them to save money for other things and build personal wealth." (Rafferty). This writer also talks about the cons that include Overcrowding of cities, Environmental Ills and very poor working conditions. We can learn from this text that the Industrial Revolution did not only benefit the rich of the time, but also many of the common people.

 With regards to literary works that speak about the conditions of the working class in during the Industrial Revolution. There was a magazine name "McClure's Magazine" that was dedicated towards "muckraking journalism". Some of the works included in the magazine where instrumental in describing the conditions at the time and aiding in the establishment of more functional laws to protect workers.

 In the magazine, there was an piece written by Ida Tarbell that talked about the unjustness and the control people with wealth had, this further strengthens the argument of the wealth disparity and lack of opportunity for the working class. She said "didn't care about anyone he did anything just to be rich and be the only company standing without any competition. He destroyed anyone else." (Tarbell) talking about John D. Rockefeller.

 In this magazine, there was also articles that depicted the lawlessness of the time. One such article is "What is a Lynching?" by Ray Stannard Baker. This text speaks about how a lynching happened. "A sort of dry rot, a moral paralysis, seems to strike the administrators of law in a town like Springfield. What can be expected of officers who are not accustomed to enforce the law, or of a people not accustomed to obey it - or who make reservations and exceptions when they do enforce it or obey it?" (Baker). Here Baker is describing how the enforcement officers are unable to make a decision as to whether or not to stop the lynching from occurring, the target was an African American man inside a jail cell. This very clearly shows the level of lawlessness experienced in big cities at the time.

Interconnection

 Were the living conditions for the working class as bad as many people believe? It's hard to say. For starters there is a lot of text about the working conditions and workplace laws, there is even a word, "muckracking", used to describe the act of outing corruption in business. However I could not find any solid statistics about the amount of people facing conditions that Jurgis experienced in "The Jungle". Although writers such as Ida Tarbell and Upton Sinclair do write accurately about working conditions, they do not provide a picture of the average or the "norm". Many people willingly moved to cities in hopes of lives better than they had previously, and many of the problems described in the literature are problems we face today. Some of these problems such as the growing wealth gap between the middle class and the wealthy, as well as the minimum wage not being able to afford a family and a good life are ones we argue about in politics all the time. However there is one thing that has improved, and that is that there is less opportunity for businesses to take advantage of workers.
